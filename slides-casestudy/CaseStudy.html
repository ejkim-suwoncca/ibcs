// Overview

What is the Case Study?

A document released by the IB before the exam that presents a real-world scenario involving current developments, emerging technologies and ethical issues in computing.

What it contains
A scenario set in a real-world context.

A list of challenges to research.

A terminology list of key vocabulary specific to the topic.
What the exam tests
Short-answer questions on terminology and key concepts.

One essay question worth half the section marks — requires analysis and independent research beyond the case study text.

Key Difference SL vs HL: SL students research 2 challenges; HL students research 4 challenges. HL has more exam time and higher essay marks.

// Exam Structure

Paper 1 — Section B

The case study is assessed in Section B of Paper 1. Short-answer questions use command terms like define, identify, outline, describe.

// Research Method

6-Step Approach · Steps 1–3

Use this systematic process to research and prepare for the case study exam.

01
Understand the text. Read the case study carefully. Highlight key points. Prepare definitions for every term in the terminology list. Quiz yourself and peers on terminology and introductory concepts.
02
Understand the technology. The case study will feature emerging technology not directly covered in the syllabus. Find video lectures and technical articles to build a solid understanding of how the technologies work. Note: no programming code required in exam responses.
03
Consider the scenario in context. With renewed understanding of the technologies, re-read the full scenario. Ask: What is this case study really about? What are the big issues? What is confusing or interesting?

Tip: Use generative AI to summarize each challenge — then fact-check every assertion independently. AI can hallucinate; treat its output as a starting point for a literature review, not the final answer.

// Research Method

6-Step Approach · Steps 4–6

04
Research each challenge. For every challenge: find background information, understand why it is a challenge in the given scenario, identify implications of not solving it, and explore potential solutions. Build a revision document summarizing findings per challenge.
05
Find real-world connections. Research and collect real-world examples related to each challenge. Add these to your revision document. This enables you to cite concrete examples in the exam beyond the case study text itself — a key marker of a proficient response.
06
Review and practise essays. Brainstorm possible exam questions based on the challenges. Write practice essays for each challenge. Swap with classmates and give feedback using the marking criteria. Prepare revision index cards with bullet-point key points.

Bonus strategies: Arrange a field trip or guest speaker. Read journal articles on arXiv.org. Watch lectures from practitioners at companies like Netflix or Spotify. Contact university researchers. The examiners reward students who demonstrate research beyond the case study document itself.

// Exam Technique

Essay Writing Strategy

The extended-response essay question is where marks are made or lost. A structured approach is essential.

Plan
Take 5 minutes to plan before writing. Any extended writing is vastly improved by brief planning. Identify 2–3 key arguments, real-world examples, and your conclusion before you start.
Term
Use terminology correctly and consistently. The examiners can immediately tell when a student is misusing vocabulary. Incorrect terminology implies poor understanding and limits marks significantly.
Analz
Analyse, don't just describe. Low-scoring responses only summarize what the case study says. Examiners already know the document. Show your independent research and use it to evaluate the challenges critically.
Bal
Provide balanced analysis. A top-band response presents multiple perspectives and draws a justified conclusion. One-sided arguments rarely score in the top band.
Cite
Reference real sources carefully. Only cite papers or publications you have actually read. Examiners are broadly familiar with major publications in the field. Do not fabricate references.
// Assessment

Essay Marking Criteria · SL

SL essay is worth 6 marks. Study the descriptors for each band.

Marks Band Description
0 — No knowledge or understanding. No use of appropriate terminology.
1–2 Basic Minimal knowledge and understanding. Minimal terminology. May be little more than a list. No reference to case study or independent research.
3–4 Adequate Descriptive response with limited understanding. Limited terminology. Some analysis. Some evidence of research.
5–6 Competent Knowledge and understanding of related issues. Terminology used appropriately. Evidence of analysis. Evidence of research. A conclusion is drawn.

To reach 5–6: Show you have researched beyond the document. Use terminology correctly. Analyse (don't just describe). Include a justified conclusion.

// Assessment

Essay Marking Criteria · HL

HL essay is worth 12 marks. The higher bands demand depth, balanced analysis, and extensive research.

Marks Band Description
1–3 Basic Minimal knowledge. Minimal terminology. Little more than a list. No reference to case study or research.
4–6 Adequate Descriptive with limited understanding. Limited terminology. Limited analysis. Limited research evident.
7–9 Competent Knowledge and understanding of related issues. Terminology used appropriately in places. Some analysis. Evidence of research undertaken.
10–12 Proficient Detailed knowledge, clear understanding. Terminology used throughout. Competent and balanced analysis. Conclusions linked to analysis. Clear evidence of extensive research.

HL Top-Band key: Balanced analysis + conclusions drawn from that analysis + clear evidence of extensive independent research. Generic, unbalanced, or description-only responses will not reach 10–12.

// Pitfalls

Common Mistakes

These patterns appear repeatedly in examiner reports across multiple years.

✕
Misusing or inventing terminology. Examiners can identify when a student is making up vocabulary. Incorrect terminology implies poor understanding and severely limits marks.
✕
Regurgitating the case study text. Examiners already know what the document says. Low-performing essays only summarize the case study. You must bring in additional insights from your own research.
✕
Lack of balanced analysis. Top-band responses present multiple perspectives before drawing a nuanced conclusion. One-sided arguments are not sufficient.
✕
Ignoring the command term. "Discuss" requires balanced argument; "evaluate" requires weighing strengths and limitations. Merely describing everything you know is not an effective strategy.
✕
Fabricating citations. Only cite publications you have actually read. Examiners are broadly familiar with major papers in each field.
✕
Over-relying on unofficial forums. Several unofficial online resources provide questionable information. Research broadly and cross-check information found.
// Top Tips

Top Tips

Know the exam structure
Know exactly what to expect before walking into the exam — structure, command terms, mark allocation. No surprises on exam day.
Field trips & guest speakers
A visit to a company or university lab related to the case study topic gives you a primary-source experience you can directly reference in your essay.
Watch practitioner talks
Search for conference talks and lectures by engineers from companies working with the case study technology. For example, Netflix engineers on recommendation systems, or robotics researchers on rescue robots.
Read journal articles
Use arXiv.org for open-access academic papers. "Students who read journal articles have a broader understanding and can employ references to real-world examples or research." — Chief Examiner, May 2024

Peer-teaching: Arrange with classmates to present on each challenge, then take questions. Your confidence when answering spontaneous questions reveals gaps in your understanding before the exam — not during it.

// Past Case Studies

Past Examples · 2020–2021

Reviewing past case studies shows the typical style, technology depth, and quality of essay responses expected.

Blockchain — May & Nov 2020, May 2021
Scenario: Mayor of Santa Monica wants a local digital currency (MONS) using blockchain.

Technologies: Digital ledgers, digital signatures, proof of work (mining), Merkle trees, blockchain structure, SHA256.

Challenges: Adding new blocks & preventing 51% attacks; scalability; cryptographic techniques; calculating balance from public ledger; disadvantages of distributed confirmation.

Sample essay question: "To what extent do you believe the MONS project will ensure the safety of residents' money?"

Chief Examiner note: Most responses covered immutability and distributed consensus but few explored blockchain's weaknesses or provided balanced analysis. Very few cited academic sources.

Takeaway: Even when students understood the technology, top marks required identifying weaknesses of the technology and presenting a balanced argument — not just explaining how it works.

// Past Case Studies

Past Examples · 2023–2024

Recommendation Systems — May & Nov 2023
Scenario: NextStar — a platform recommending undiscovered artists to users based on ratings.

Technologies: Content-based filtering, collaborative filtering, k-nearest neighbours, matrix factorization, training and evaluation of recommender systems.

Chief Examiner: "Most candidates focused on content-based and collaborative filtering with little evaluation. Few referenced the case study scenario (NextStar) — generic movie-recommendation examples instead. Very few referenced journal articles."
Rescue Robots — May & Nov 2024
Scenario: BotPro rescue robots using computer vision to assist in disaster-response situations.

Technologies: Computer vision, vSLAM (visual simultaneous localization and mapping), pose estimation.

Chief Examiner: Strong responses connected historical examples (9/11) to current uses (Ukraine), demonstrating how the technology had evolved over 20 years.

Pattern across all years: Students who cite real-world examples, reference academic papers, and provide balanced analysis consistently score in the higher bands. Descriptive, case-study-only responses remain in the adequate band.
