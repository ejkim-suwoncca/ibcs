// Why Ethics?

Why Does ML Ethics Matter?

Machine learning systems make decisions that directly affect people's lives — in hiring, healthcare, law enforcement, finance and more. The data they learn from reflects the world, including its biases.

ML Decisions Affect Lives
Models approve or deny loans, flag criminal risk, filter job applications, and diagnose diseases. Errors or bias in these systems can have profound, lasting consequences.
Training Data Reflects Reality
If historical data contains prejudice, a model trained on it learns and amplifies that prejudice. It won't automatically produce fair outcomes — it reproduces patterns in the data.
Scale and Speed
Unlike a single human decision, ML systems can affect millions of people simultaneously. A biased algorithm can discriminate at a scale no individual could.
Opacity
Many ML models (especially deep learning) are "black boxes" — even engineers cannot always explain why a specific decision was made, making accountability difficult.
// Ethical Issue 1 & 2

Accountability & Algorithmic Bias

①
Accountability — who is responsible when an ML system causes harm?
The company that built the AI? The organization that deployed it? The individual who used it? Often the answer is unclear.

2018 Self-Driving Car Fatality: A self-driving car struck and killed a pedestrian. The human backup driver was sentenced to probation. But questions remained: should Uber (the developer) have faced greater liability? How do we assign fault when the decision-maker is an algorithm?

②
Algorithmic Fairness & Bias — ML can inherit and amplify social biases from training data
Over- or under-representation of demographic groups in training data leads to unfair model predictions.

COMPAS Recidivism Tool: Used in US courts to predict re-offending. Studies found it falsely flagged Black defendants as high-risk at twice the rate of white defendants, despite similar actual re-offending rates.

Amazon Recruiting Tool (2018): Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool that was systematically biased against women — because it was trained on résumés submitted over 10 years, which were predominantly from men.

Key Insight: Bias can enter at every stage — data collection (who was surveyed?), feature selection (which attributes matter?), and model design (what outcome are we optimizing for?).

// Ethical Issue 3 & 4

Consent & Privacy

③
Consent — large training datasets regularly contain information collected without explicit consent
Photos, posts, health records, and browsing habits are collected and used for ML training, often without the knowledge of the individuals involved.

Google DeepMind & NHS (2016): DeepMind received 1.6 million patient records from a UK hospital to develop a kidney-injury detection app. An investigation found patients had not been adequately informed — a breach of UK privacy law.

④
Privacy — ML systems can predict or infer highly personal information that was never directly provided
Health conditions, political beliefs, sexual orientation, and financial vulnerability can all be inferred from seemingly innocuous data (location, purchases, browsing).

Strava Heat Map (2018): Fitness-tracking app Strava published a global activity heat map. The movement patterns of users (including military personnel) inadvertently revealed the locations of secret military bases and patrol routes in conflict zones.

Think: Who owns your data? Should users be able to opt out of ML training that uses their data? How can "informed consent" work at scale when most people don't read privacy policies?

// Ethical Issue 5 & 6

Security & Environmental Impact

⑤
Security — ML systems are vulnerable to a range of adversarial attacks
Data Poisoning
Injecting harmful or false data into the training set to manipulate the model's behaviour for malicious purposes.
Model Evasion
Crafting inputs (e.g. adversarial images or prompts) that trick the model into producing incorrect or harmful outputs ("jailbreaking").
Model Inversion
Reconstructing sensitive training data from model outputs — a privacy attack on the data used to train the model.

Microsoft Tay Bot (2016): Within 24 hours of release, users deliberately fed Microsoft's AI chatbot offensive content. Tay learned from these interactions and began producing racist and harmful tweets. Microsoft shut it down within a day.

⑥
Environmental Impact — training large ML models consumes enormous energy
Carbon Cost of Training
Training GPT-3 consumed electricity equivalent to ~500 metric tons of carbon. Global data centre power use is forecast to exceed 1,000 TWh by 2026.
Questions to Consider
Is the benefit of a model worth its environmental cost? Should model efficiency be an ethical criterion? How do we balance technological progress with sustainability?
// Ethical Issue 7 & 8

Transparency & Societal Impact

⑦
Transparency — many ML systems cannot explain their own decisions
The "black box" problem: decisions made by complex neural networks are often impossible to interpret or audit.

Apple Card Credit Limits (2019): Tech entrepreneur David Heinemeier Hansson reported on social media that Apple Card offered him 20× the credit limit of his wife, despite them having shared assets and her having a higher credit score. The algorithm's reasoning could not be explained.

⑧
Misinformation & Online Bias — ML can generate and amplify false or biased content at scale
Misinformation
Generative AI can create convincing fake text, images, audio, and video ("deepfakes"). Studies suggest misinformation received 6× more clicks than factual news during the 2020 US election (NYU study).
Echo Chambers
Recommendation algorithms (YouTube, Facebook) are designed to maximize engagement — this leads to filter bubbles where users see content reinforcing existing beliefs, potentially polarizing public opinion.

IB Command Term: When discussing these issues, always consider both sides. Example: "Echo chambers are harmful to democracy, BUT recommendation systems also help users discover relevant content. The ethical issue is how to balance engagement with exposure to diverse viewpoints."

// A4.4.2 · Emerging Technologies

Reassessing Ethics as Technology Advances

Society must continuously revisit ethical frameworks as new technologies emerge. The following are key areas of concern:

⚛ Quantum Computing
Quantum computers could break current cryptographic systems (RSA, ECC), threatening the security of internet communications, banking, and personal data. Development of quantum-resistant cryptography is urgent.
🥽 Augmented Reality (AR)
AR devices collect vast amounts of data about users' physical environments and movements. Ethical concerns: Who owns this data? Does altering perceived reality disconnect people from society and reduce empathy?
🎮 Virtual Reality (VR)
As VR becomes increasingly realistic: what are the mental health implications of excessive use or escapism? What limits should exist for VR content involving violence, exploitation, or explicit material?
🤖 Pervasive AI
AI is embedded in everyday life (smart assistants, surveillance, recommendation). Concerns: continuous data collection, loss of autonomy, surveillance capitalism, and equity of access across different socio-economic groups.

Core Principle: The pace of technological change outstrips current ethical and legal frameworks. We need proactive, adaptive governance — not just reactive regulation after harm has occurred.

// A4.4.2 Continued

Privacy & Equity in an AI-Driven World

🔒
Data Ownership: Who owns the data about you — you, or the company that collected it?
As collection becomes more complex, informed consent is becoming almost impossible to achieve meaningfully. Most privacy policies are deliberately difficult to understand.
⚖
Equity & Access: How do we ensure technology reduces — rather than magnifies — inequality?
Disparities across socio-economic, racial, gender, and geographic groups mean that AI systems can entrench existing inequalities. Who benefits from AI advances — and who bears the costs?
Questions to Consider
• Should AI be used in healthcare when not all populations are equally represented in training data?
• Should AI hiring tools be permitted if they cannot be proven unbiased?
• Who is responsible for ensuring equitable access to AI-driven services?
Approaches to Address Inequity
• Diverse training data — intentional inclusion of underrepresented groups
• Algorithmic audits — regular testing for discriminatory outcomes
• Regulation — legal frameworks requiring fairness (e.g. EU AI Act)
• Community inclusion — involving affected communities in AI design

IB Exam Tip: When discussing equity, reference both the problem (e.g. biased facial recognition performs worse on darker skin tones) and potential solutions (e.g. diverse training data, independent audits). Show you can evaluate trade-offs.

// Exam Technique

How to "Discuss" Ethics in IB Exams

The command term Discuss requires a balanced argument, not just a list of problems.

1
Identify the ethical issue clearly
Name the specific principle at stake (e.g. "Privacy is at risk because…")
2
Provide a specific, real-world example
Vague answers lose marks. Use case studies (COMPAS, Strava, Tay, Apple Card, DeepMind/NHS, Amazon recruiting).
3
Present multiple perspectives
Consider the interests of: the individual affected, the company, regulators, society as a whole.
4
Propose a counterargument or mitigation
Show awareness that most ethical dilemmas don't have simple answers. What trade-offs are involved?
5
Reach a reasoned conclusion
A "discuss" answer should end with a justified position — not just restating the problem.

Common Mistake: Don't oversimplify ("AI is bad for privacy") or confuse technical bias (model accuracy errors) with ethical bias (unfair treatment of groups). The IB expects nuanced, evidence-based discussion.
