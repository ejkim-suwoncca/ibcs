Part 1 · Basics

AI vs LLM — What's the difference?

🤖 AI (Artificial Intelligence)
  • A broad term for machines that can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence
  • Includes image recognition, self-driving cars, chess engines, voice assistants
  • AI is the category
💬 LLM (Large Language Model)
  • A specific type of AI trained on massive amounts of text
  • Designed to understand and generate human language
  • Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini
  • LLM is a tool inside AI
📌 Every LLM is AI — but not every AI is an LLM. Think of it like: every square is a rectangle, but not every rectangle is a square.

Part 1 · How it works

How does an LLM generate text?

An LLM doesn't "think" or "know" things the way humans do. It predicts — based on patterns it learned from billions of web pages, books, and articles.

"The sky"
is → 72%
looks → 18%
was → 10%
"The sky is"
blue → 65%
clear → 20%
the limit → 15%
💡 The model picks the most likely next word — over and over — until it finishes. That's why your prompt is so powerful: it sets the direction of all those predictions.

Context shapes everything

Because an LLM predicts based on context, the information you give it directly changes the quality of the output.

VariableWhat it affectsEffect
Training dataWhat the model learnedSets its base knowledge and style
Your promptThe context for this conversationMore detail = better, more relevant output
Word choiceWhich patterns are triggeredOne word can completely change the response
📌 More information. More specific context. More precise words. → Better results. This is exactly why prompt engineering matters.

Part 2 · What is a Prompt?

Weak vs. Strong Prompts

❌ Weak Prompt
"Help me with my website."
→ Too vague. What kind of help? What website? What have you tried? The AI can only guess.
✅ Strong Prompt
"I'm a year 11 IB Computer Science student building a to-do list web app. My HTML and CSS are done but the JavaScript to add new tasks isn't working. Can you review my code and explain what's wrong?"
→ Specific context → precise, useful answer.
💡 An LLM doesn't "understand" your situation — it only knows what you tell it. Give it more, and it gives you more.
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 09
Part 2 · Analogy

LLM is like a very well-read intern

An intern who…
  • Has read thousands of books and websites
  • Is eager to help
  • Doesn't know your project yet
  • Will do exactly what you ask — even if it's the wrong thing
  • → Needs a clear briefing
An LLM who…
  • Has learned from billions of texts
  • Responds to every prompt instantly
  • Has no memory of your previous work
  • Produces what's statistically likely, not necessarily what's right
  • → Needs a clear prompt
📌 The better your brief, the better your intern's work. The better your prompt, the better the AI's output.

Part 3 · RICE Overview

The RICE Framework at a glance

R
Role
Tell the AI who it should be. Assign a persona, expertise level, or perspective to shape the tone and depth of its answer.
I
Instructions
Tell the AI exactly what to do. Be specific about the task, the format, the length, and any constraints.
C
Context
Give the AI the background it needs. Who are you? What's the situation? What has already happened? What is this for?
E
Examples
Show the AI what good looks like. One example is worth a hundred words of description. Paste in a sample or describe the style you want.
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 12
Part 3 · RICE

R — Role

Tell the AI who it should be. This sets the persona, expertise, and tone.

Without Role
"Explain object-oriented programming."
→ Generic answer. Could be for anyone, any level.
With Role
Role You are a patient IB Computer Science teacher explaining to a Year 11 student for the first time.
→ Approachable tone, right level of depth, relevant vocabulary.
💡 Try roles like: "experienced developer reviewing code", "examiner giving IB-style feedback", "classmate explaining simply"
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 13
R
Part 3 · RICE

I — Instructions

Tell the AI exactly what you want it to do. Be specific about task, format, length, and constraints.

Vague Instructions
"Write something about my website."
→ What to write? How long? For who? In what style?
Precise Instructions
Instructions Write a 3-sentence introduction for my portfolio website. Use a professional but friendly tone. Do not use the word "passionate".
→ Length ✓ Tone ✓ Specific constraint ✓
💡 Include: what to produce, what format (list/table/paragraph/code), what length, what to avoid, what to include.
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 14
I
Part 3 · RICE

C — Context

Give the AI the background it needs to understand your situation. The AI only knows what you tell it.

No Context
"Why isn't my login working?"
→ The AI has no idea what you've built, what language you're using, or what the error says.
Rich Context
Context I'm a Year 11 IB CS student. My website uses Node.js + Express for the backend and plain HTML/CSS/JS for the front end. When I click the login button, nothing happens and the console shows "TypeError: Cannot read properties of null."
→ Stack, symptom, error message all provided. AI can actually help.
💡 Context checklist: Who are you? What's the goal? What have you tried? What went wrong? What tools/languages are you using?
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 15
C
Part 3 · RICE

E — Examples

Show the AI what good output looks like. One example teaches more than ten sentences of description.

No Example
"Write a comment explaining this function."
→ The AI doesn't know the style, format, or detail level you want.
With Example
Example Here's the style I want: // Checks if the username already exists in the database. Returns true if found, false otherwise. — Now write one for the function below: [paste code]
→ AI matches your exact style and detail level.
💡 You can show examples of: writing style, code style, layout, tone, answer format, or what you don't want ("avoid responses like this: …")

A full RICE prompt — website assessment

Same goal — completely different results

❌ Without RICE
"Help me write my reflection."

AI response: Generic, surface-level. Could apply to any student doing any project.
✅ With RICE
Role IB CS examiner reviewing a student report.
Instructions Help me write a Reflection section (200 words). Focus on what I learned and what I'd do differently.
Context I built a to-do list web app. I struggled with connecting the frontend to the backend using fetch(). I fixed it by learning about async/await.
Examples "I underestimated the complexity of…" — that kind of honest, reflective tone.

AI response: Specific, personal, on-brief. Sounds like you.

Part 4 · Iteration

Prompt → Review → Refine

Your first prompt is rarely your best. Treat it like a first draft.

1
Send your RICE prompt
Use Role, Instructions, Context, and Examples as a starting point.
2
Review the output
Is it the right level? Right tone? Is anything missing or wrong?
3
Refine your prompt
Add what was missing: "Make it shorter", "Avoid jargon", "Focus only on X".
4
Verify & own it
Test the code. Check the facts. Rewrite it in your own voice. You're responsible for what you submit.
💡 You can also say: "That's good, but make it 30% shorter" or "Rewrite using simpler vocabulary" — follow-up prompts are powerful.
Prompt Engineering · Student Guide 20
Part 4 · Academic Integrity

Using AI responsibly in your assessment

✅
AI is allowed — and must be documented Keep records of every prompt you used and the output it gave. This is part of your submission evidence.
🔍
You must understand everything you submit If AI wrote code or text for you and you can't explain it, that's a problem. Use AI to learn, not to skip learning.
⚠️
AI can be wrong (hallucination) LLMs sometimes produce confident-sounding but incorrect information or code that doesn't work. Always test and verify.
✍️
Your voice, your report AI can help you structure or improve your writing — but your report should reflect your thinking, your design decisions, your reflection.
